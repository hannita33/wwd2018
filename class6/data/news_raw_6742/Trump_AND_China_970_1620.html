The Start of a New Arms Race?


SECTION: Section A; Column 0; Editorial Desk; LETTERS; Pg. 22


LENGTH: 659 words


To the Editor:

Re ''Kindling Talk of an Arms Race With a Vow to 'Outlast Them All' '' (front page, Dec. 24):

Will someone explain to President-elect Donald Trump, who has threatened to ''outmatch'' rivals in a new nuclear arms race, that one cannot ''win'' such a race? That even the ''losers'' in the race accumulate nuclear weapons sufficient to end civilization as we know it, and that therefore the measure of an American president's success in nuclear strategy is his ability to discourage such races from occurring in the first place?

To encourage a global nuclear arms race is simply to multiply the nations that pose an existential threat to the United States, dramatically decreasing American national security. This is an elementary point of American nuclear strategy, accepted by all recent presidents. One very much hopes that there are level heads around the president-elect with the wherewithal to explain it. 

DAVID CIEPLEY

Denver

The writer is an associate professor of political science at the University of Denver.

To the Editor:

Re ''Mr. Trump Flirts With an Arms Race'' (editorial, Dec. 24):

I am going to do something I never thought I would and defend President-elect Donald Trump on this one, as some Americans are tone deaf to the code in his comments that Trump voters seem to understand. Mr. Trump's aggressive response to a comment from President Vladimir Putin about increasing Russia's nuclear capability was almost certainly designed to avoid an arms race, not start one.

DEBORAH MORAN

Houston

To the Editor:

Both political parties and the courts should cooperate to keep from office (or remove from office) a president-elect whose mind, emotions and behavior endanger national and global security. The grounds could relate to his taxes, his foundation, his university, conflicts of interest or the Emoluments Clause of the Constitution. He would command a superpower but, with no experience in government or world politics, he shuns intelligence briefings and dismisses the accumulated wisdom of Foreign Service professionals.

His defenders may say that breaking with precedent, as on ''one China,'' does not create a clear and present danger. Nor does flirting with Russia or -- in the opposite direction -- acquiring more nukes. But the man's temperament could lead him to impetuous acts that trigger a nuclear war, initiated by the United States or by one of its adversaries.

At the same time, steps should be taken to create a firewall to prevent any president from launching a nuclear weapon without a mechanism for consultation with appropriate authorities. The danger is not that deterrence will fail but that passions may overwhelm rationality.

WALTER C. CLEMENS Jr.

Sudbury, Mass.

The writer is an associate at the Davis Center for Russian and Eurasian Studies at Harvard University.

To the Editor:

President-elect Donald Trump's pledge on nuclear arms to ''outmatch them at every pass and outlast them all'' has struck listeners not so much as a policy statement as a reflexive expression of national machismo. It seems that to Mr. Trump, everything is a competition, and if he's not the winner, he's a loser.

With that attitude, it will be all too easy for international leaders to bluff him into risky, expensive actions that waste America's resources without serving our interests. Is Donald Trump falling into the same sort of trap that Ronald Reagan laid for Vladimir Putin's predecessors in the old Soviet Union, egged down the path to ruinous competition for a pointless prize? Is Donald Trump's America going to be bamboozled into bankruptcy?

JESSA MITTLEMAN

Croton, N.Y.

To the Editor:

Each time Donald Trump has tweeted about nuclear weapons, I have had a terrible, familiar feeling: the anxiety I experienced as a child in the 1950s, thinking that the world could end in a moment. That children would have to live with that again is horrifying to me.

JANE S. MORSE

Laurel, Md.
