Hillary Clinton Takes Aim at North Korea, Then at Donald Trump


BYLINE: By AMY CHOZICK


SECTION: Section ; Column 0; Politics; Pg.


LENGTH: 578 words


Hillary Clinton called Friday for rethinking President Obama's strategy to combat North Korea's nuclear program, saying the country's latest nuclear test gave the White House ''an opening'' to expand sanctions and broaden a missile-defense program in neighboring countries.

She also said that the United States can do more to pressure China to lean on North Korea, its longtime ally, to halt its program.  

''It's clear that the increasing threat posed by North Korea requires not only a rethinking of the strategy, but an urgent effort to convince the neighbors, most particularly China, that this is not just a U.S. issue,'' Mrs. Clinton said, signaling she would support a change in strategy since she served as secretary of state.

Since the start of the Obama administration, Mrs. Clinton had supported Mr. Obama on his ''strategic patience'' approach of gradually escalating sanctions on North Korea, but the blast on Friday signaled that the tactic had failed to halt North Korean advances in developing its nuclear capacity.

On Friday, Mrs. Clinton convened a bipartisan group of foreign policy advisers in New York, including the former Homeland Security secretaries Michael Chertoff and Janet Napolitano, and David H. Petraeus, the former director of the C.I.A., to discuss the fight to combat the Islamic State and stop homegrown terrorism.

But the news of North Korea's latest test quickly shifted the conversation to Asia and, specifically, relations with China.

''I think we have an opening here that we haven't had for the last seven years,'' Mrs. Clinton told reporters at the New-York Historical Society after the meeting.

The missile defense system that the United States is planning with South Korea, she said, ''is not something that the North Koreans or the Chinese or the Russians in the region are particularly pleased about -- but what is the alternative?''

Mr. Obama has resisted imposing a full embargo against North Korea, which could provoke a deeper conflict. But Mrs. Clinton said sanctions alone were not enough, pointing to the advances in nuclear technology made by Iran despite sanctions. ''They were able to acquire and put into operation a significant number of centrifuges,'' she said.

But, she added, tightening existing sanctions was one essential step.

''There's lot more we can do,'' she said, ''And it will be on the top of my list in dealing with China in how we are going to prevent what could very well be a serious conflict with North Korea.''

As the news conference concluded and Mrs. Clinton started to exit, a reporter yelled out for her opinion on Donald J. Trump's appearance on Russian television. Mrs. Clinton grinned and made her way back to the podium, eager to speak on the topic.

''This just becomes more and more of a reality television show,'' she said. ''It's not a serious presidential campaign.''

Mr. Trump also addressed the North Korean nuclear blast, laying the blame for North Korea's behavior squarely at the feet of Mrs. Clinton.

''Just today, it was announced that North Korea performed its fifth nuclear test -- its fourth since Hillary Clinton became secretary of state,'' he told a meeting of the Family Research Council in Washington. ''It's just one more massive failure from a failed secretary of state. Failed at everything.''
