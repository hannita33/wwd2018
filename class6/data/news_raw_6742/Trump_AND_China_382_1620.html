Rubio and Cruz Leave Trump Sputtering


BYLINE: ELIZABETH WILLIAMSON


SECTION: OPINION


LENGTH: 345 words



HIGHLIGHT: Mr. Rubio and Mr. Cruz questioned the Republican front-runner&#8217;s ideas, ethics and conservative credentials at Thursday&#8217;s debate.


Well that was fun. And why didn't it happen months ago?

Marco Rubio and Ted Cruz went hammer and tongs after Donald Trump at Thursday night's Republican debate in Houston, questioning his ideas, ethics, business practices, and conservative credentials. Marco Rubio led the charge, grinning broadly as he lambasted Mr. Trump for hiring undocumented workers, making his Trump-branded clothing in China, and failing to release his tax returns. Scoffing at Mr. Trump's outsized boasts about his wealth, Mr. Rubio said: "If he hadn't inherited $200 million, you know where Donald Trump would be right now? Selling watches in Manhattan." The audience erupted in laughter. 

Interestingly, one of the most sustained, resonant attacks was on an issue that's been out there for months: multiple lawsuits against Mr. Trump's so-called "Trump University." Mr. Rubio called the enterprise a "fake university."

"There are people that borrowed $36,000 to go to Trump University, and they're suing now," he said. "And you know what they got? They got to take a picture with a cardboard cutout of Donald Trump."

Mr. Cruz jumped in, asking voters to consider what it would be like to have Mr. Trump as the G.O.P. nominee, testifying as a defendant in a multi-million-dollar fraud case.

Google searches of "Trump University" went through the roof.

Mr. Trump was left sputtering, with nothing to say.

Pressed repeatedly on when he'd release his tax returns, Mr. Trump, who has resisted doing so, found a new excuse. He is being audited, and can't release the returns until the audit is complete (which of course he could.) After the debate, he suggested that he gets audited repeatedly because of his religion. As Mr. Trump himself would say (on Twitter): Bad!

Mr. Rubio even found time to ridicule Mr. Trump for repeating himself, and for his near-total lack of substance.

All in all, this last debate before the crucial Super Tuesday primaries on March 1 was a delightful, long-overdue trouncing of Mr. Trump. Whether it will cut into his substantial lead is, of course, the race's biggest question.
