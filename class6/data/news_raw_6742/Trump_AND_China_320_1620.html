The Stakes in the Election


SECTION: Section SR; Column 0; Editorial Desk; SUNDAY DIALOGUE; Pg. 10


LENGTH: 1985 words


Readers discuss what a President Trump would mean for America's future.

To the Editor:

Re ''In a Narrow Race, Many View Trump as a Change Agent'' (front page, Sept. 16) and ''The Black Eyes in Trump's Life,'' by Nicholas Kristof (column, Sept. 8):

It seems that there's no end to the list of ugly words and deeds in Donald Trump's life. What's more, many are already well known, yet he is rising in the polls for reasons no one seems to fully understand. True, the growing economy has left many behind, and ISIS is still a serious threat, but why would anyone think the solution is electing a self-important fraud who knows nothing of government and boasts of a chummy relationship with a head of state who's crushed freedom in his own country?

The larger concern this raises is about our own political system. If our democracy cannot deny the nation's highest office to someone so deeply bigoted, uninformed and untrustworthy as Donald Trump, could we be looking at the twilight of democracy itself? Loss of freedom in other countries began the same way, with self-absorbed leaders with similar beliefs and personality traits. 

It's ironic to be questioning our democracy after the highly successful two terms of our first black president and the first major-party nomination of a woman, but I have to believe that a great many voters do not appreciate how much is at stake for America's future in this election.

DAVID KERNIS

Trumbull, Conn.

The writer is a retired corporate communications executive.

Readers React

I share Mr. Kernis's fears about Donald Trump, but I don't think his candidacy portends the ''twilight of our democracy.'' Mr. Trump is an affront to a great many Americans but not, obviously, to all. Be that as it may, it is the very nature of democracy that enables a candidate as grievously unqualified by experience and temperament to grab the baton and run with it. As Winston Churchill observed, democracy is the worst form of government except for all the others. The founders knew this, and established a divided government of checks and balances that for more than 200 years has withstood constitutional crises and survived: the Civil War, F.D.R.'s attempted packing of the Supreme Court, Watergate, to name just a few. We have also suffered and survived a fair number of poor or disastrous presidents. This is not to underestimate the damage Mr. Trump could wreak. The choice has never before been clearer. In the end, no matter how unenthusiastic one may be about Hillary Clinton, she is the better choice. Those disappointed and disaffected by the menu offered are still obligated to come to the table and partake of what is available. The only way to keep America ''great,'' whatever that means, is to ensure that we do not elect Donald Trump.

NICHOLAS W. PUNER

West Tisbury, Mass.

I believe that many voters of all ages are asking themselves whether we are looking at the ''twilight of democracy,'' as Mr. Kernis suggests in his thoughtful letter. Just a few short months ago, sitting with thousands of others at a Bernie Sanders rally in New Brunswick, N.J., amid chants of ''this is what democracy looks like,'' I believed we were on the cusp of a better time in our democratic growth and maturation. Perhaps the lesson in both the Donald Trump and Bernie Sanders campaigns is that democracy remains a work in progress, a precarious and delicate work to be sure, and one that could produce a leader on the foundation of profound hatred and prejudice just as quickly as one who proffers compassion and inclusion. The future of American democracy clings less on the outcome of this election and more on the lessons we learn from the present moment.

PETER SCHMIDT

Phillipsburg, N.J.

When historians look back on the decline of democracy in America, I suspect they will identify two contributing factors. One is the shallow and faulty information transmitted in the majority of news media. The second is citizens' lack of knowledge of American political history. Without solid factual information and lacking an education in government and history, voters cannot make sensible decisions and are easily bamboozled by demagogues -- clearly the situation in America today. Democracy cannot function with an ill-informed, ignorant and politically illiterate electorate.

JOSEPHINE DONOVAN

Urbana, Ill.

The Donald Trump debacle is reminiscent of the rise of totalitarian figures, who woo the disaffected by making them think that someone is taking something away from them that is rightfully theirs and that a show of brute strength in a leader is more important than the truth of what he says. Democracy will survive in America because of the checks and balances of a strong two-party system, but the public face and the heart and soul of our nation, as perceived at home and abroad, could suffer a horrific blow if this ignorant man should reach its highest office.

BERNARD LANGS

New Providence, N.J.

Many reasons explain the popularity of candidates such as Donald Trump, but one that stands out is the increasing distrust we feel toward our institutions and toward one another, as well documented in recent surveys. This in turn leads to a lowered sense of community and a weakening of our democracy. The way forward, no matter who wins the election, is to rebuild trust and community feeling. This can best begin on a local level. All of us, leaders and community members alike, have a necessary role to play in creating cohesive, inclusive, sustainable and participatory local community life -- which is the keystone of democratic self-rule, and of democracy itself.

BILL BERKOWITZ

Arlington, Mass.

The writer, emeritus professor of psychology at University of Massachusetts Lowell, is a founding contributor to the Community Tool Box, a community building website.

Thinking about what is at stake for America's future is very difficult when a citizen is preoccupied with what is at stake for his or her own future. It is debatable as to whether Donald Trump has done a service to the country by ''letting out the dogs,'' but he is forcing us to see who we really are as a nation. Actually, this election is democracy in action; now let's see if it really works.

MICHAEL SCHWARTZ

Dix Hills, N.Y.

Blaming Donald Trump for the demise of the American democracy and for his own popularity seems incongruous. With our legislature composed mostly of millionaires, elected through a gerrymandered system, funded by a precious few and managed by an established elite, Mr. Trump's supporters -- and Bernie Sanders's -- may represent a desperate last gasp to reaffirm true democracy. That being said, Mr. Trump scares the hell out of me. But he is a symptom of a failing republic, not the cause.

ROBERT READ THRUN

Covington, Ky.

Democracy requires a well-informed and engaged citizenry to function properly. Unfortunately American voters are updated about politics and current affairs by media outlets increasingly intent on ratings and financial performance. As a result, much of the political discourse on television is little more than partisan entertainment. Ironically, some of our best political pundits are comedians. Donald Trump, the reality TV star, has taken full advantage of this media circus. Whatever you may think about Mr. Trump, you must admit he has manipulated the media masterfully. He consistently engages in outrageous behavior, which would disqualify other candidates, to obtain vast amounts of free publicity. This is not the twilight of democracy. Rather, it is the dawn of reality-show entertainment politics.

JERRY WALLINGFORD

San Diego

In ''Why So Many Business Leaders Are Tiptoeing Around Trump'' (DealBook column, Sept. 20), Andrew Ross Sorkin describes conversations with many chief executives who criticize Donald Trump but add, ''I could never say that on the record'' because he could bully them, their families and their companies. Self-silencing because of fear is a hallmark of societies under dictatorships. Yes, our democracy will be endangered if Mr. Trump is elected. Merely contemplating the possibility, some Americans are already less free.

JENNY GOLUB

New York

In recent memory, we survived Watergate and the re-election of George W. Bush. Our institutions are strong, and our faith in them keeps us from tearing up flagstones and throwing bombs rather than waiting for the next election cycle. Much as I abhor the notion, should Donald Trump win, we may rationally hope that in four, or at most eight, years, we will have a new president as different from him as President Obama was from George W. Bush. This dialectic process is messy, but I hope and believe that it will continue.

A lot of Mr. Trump's support is anarchic, based on the notion that the status quo is so awful that any alternative, no matter how risky, is preferable. Unless pigs fly in a Trump presidency and he really grows in the job, which I do not read as a good bet, his supporters will soon learn that he has nothing to offer beyond narcissistic pomposity. They will see yesterday's bad become today's awful. There will be a big swing in the other direction toward, possibly, the next Bernie Sanders.

HAROLD BROWN

New York

Yes, Donald Trump would usher in the twilight of democracy. While most of the media attention has been devoted to Mr. Trump's bullying, sleazy business history,and outrageous statements, what he says he will do if elected is even more frightening. He is going to build ''the wall'' and forcibly relocate millions of undocumented residents; reduce Chinese imports, which would likely trigger a trade war; keep Muslims from entering the country; roll back environmental regulations; eliminate Obamacare; and appoint pro-life Supreme Court justices. The list goes on.

While Mr. Trump's appeal as an agent for change explains much of his support, if his agenda becomes reality, America would fundamentally change in dangerous ways. We have seen radical change before -- in Russia 100 years ago, in Germany in the 1930s and in China in the 1940s. We have also seen strongmen. They only get worse when in power. ''The twilight of democracy'' is staring us in the face.

JOE HOWELL

Washington

The Writer Responds

Like Mr. Puner and other respondents, I have strong faith in our democracy. Democracy is also ''a work in progress,'' as Mr. Schmidt says, and needs constant attention. But money talks in America, perhaps louder than ever, making it even more difficult to give ordinary Americans a voice. Electing the rich, self-indulgent Donald Trump, who has little regard for rules and regulations that don't suit him, and no regard for people who don't adore him, would be a calamity for our democracy that could take decades to repair. Mr. Puner and others say they are confident that our democracy can withstand the challenge, but the prospect of a Trump presidency, along with a compliant Congress and a Supreme Court with Trump appointees, leaves me deeply concerned. Our strong democracy notwithstanding, prudence dictates that we do all we can to keep Donald Trump out of the White House.

This may also be the election that mandates a deeper understanding of the American people. Mr. Berkowitz says that the distrust we feel toward one another and toward our institutions weakens our democracy. I agree that many Americans have ceased to see our nation as one community. That is surely more difficult as America becomes more diverse, ethnically, racially and religiously. But we need to try. I believe that our Constitution points the way. We are all equal under the law. I believe that we can recover our sense of one nation indivisible if our institutions, schools, churches and others recognize their responsibility to help bring us together.

DAVID KERNIS

Trumbull, Conn.
