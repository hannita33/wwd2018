Trump, Syria, Schumer: Your Wednesday Evening Briefing


BYLINE: KAREN ZRAICK and SANDRA STEVENSON


SECTION: BRIEFING


LENGTH: 854 words



HIGHLIGHT: Here's what you need to know at the end of the day.


(Want to get this briefing by email? Here's the sign-up.)

Good evening. Here's the latest.

1. The world will have to wait a bit longer to see who President-elect Donald Trump appoints to his cabinet.

The process has been slowed by a series of shake-ups at Trump Tower, which has also virtually frozen the handoff of critical information from the current administration. Mr. Trump has played down the disarray. 

There wasplenty of disarray outside the tower, too. (See above.)

The front-runner for secretary of state, Rudolph Giuliani, is facing questions about whether his business ties should disqualify him.

Syria's president, Bashar al-Assad, whose country has been torn apart by a five-year war, called Mr. Trump "a natural ally" in the fight against terrorism.

____

2. The Democrats are taking a "big tent" approach as they try to recover from the election.

In the Senate, they chose Chuck Schumer, above, as the incoming minority leader, and announced a 10-member leadership team.

The new team includes Bernie Sanders and Elizabeth Warren, but also figures like Joe Manchin, the blue-collar conservative from West Virginia.

____

3. President Obama met with Chancellor Angela Merkel of Germany as part of his last international tour as president.

During his first stop, in Greece, he visited the Acropolis, above, and told an audience in Athens that "American democracy is bigger than any one person."

He heads to Peru next.

____

4. The police officer who killed Philando Castile, the African-American driver whose graphic death in Minnesota was streamed live on Facebook, was charged with second-degree manslaughter.

Mr. Castile's death attracted almost immediate international attention, largely because of the video, filmed by Mr. Castile's distraught girlfriend.

The officer, Jeronimo Yanez, is set to appear in court on Friday.

____

5. Bob Dylan will not attend the ceremony for his Nobel Prize in Literature.

The Swedish Academy, which gives out the award, said Mr. Dylan told it he had "pre-existing commitments" on Dec. 10, the day of the ceremony.

"He underscored, once again, that he feels very honored indeed, wishing that he could receive the prize in person," the academy said.

It was unclear what the prior commitment was. Mr. Dylan's website lists no tour dates past Nov. 23.

____

6. The International Energy Agency said the landmark climate accord reached in Paris almost a year ago is too weak to meet its temperature target.

Mr. Obama says he will try to persuade Mr. Trump not to quit the deal.

Melting glaciers, like the one in China, above, have been one of the most visible harbingers of climate change.

___

7. The protests over an oil pipeline in Standing Rock, N.D., are the most visible sign of an emerging movement.

Native American tribes across the U.S. and Canada are linking up to demand a greater say in how to manage public land and protect the environment.

"What it comes down to is our cultural history, and the land that we've been handed down by our ancestors to take care of," said one leader.

___

8. Two studies published in the science journal Nature show how the surface of Pluto might have been scarred long ago when a smaller body slammed into it.

The research was spurred by images captured by a NASA spacecraft last year that showed a bright heart-shaped splotch on Pluto's surface.

____

9. Despite what the ads say, the overwhelming majority of deals on the day after Thanksgiving are duds.

The sales are mainly used by retailers to clear out unwanted goods. The best-selling products rarely drop much in price.

We teamed up again with The Wirecutter, a website for product recommendations that was recently acquired by The New York Times, to weed out the good deals from the bad.

____

10. So many tourists are visiting Iceland that the locals are expected to be outnumbered by seven to one next year.

Two very unlikely events made it a red-hot travel destination: The currency collapse after the 2008 recession made it cheap, and a volcanic explosion two years later put it on the map.

(It's also famous for its horses; some rich visitors even buy them.)

"Iceland has been saved by the crash and the eruption," said one resort owner. "I have never seen anything take off so fast."

____

11. Finally, we've been talking a lot about how to get ready for Thanksgiving. Mostly it's been recipes. But you may also be preparing for political arguments with friends or relatives.

Here are some tips on how to argue better: listen, don't nitpick, and try to understand opposing points of view.

Don't think of an argument as an opportunity to convince the other person of your view; think of it as a way to test and improve your opinions.
