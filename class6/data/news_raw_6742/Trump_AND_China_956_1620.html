Good morning.

Here's what you need to know:

The Berlin Christmas market where 12 people died Monday night was the target of a terrorist attack, Chancellor Angela Merkel said today.

The police searched a refugee shelter at Tempelhof airport as part of their investigation into the attack. A man suspected of driving the truck into the crowded market is in custody.

Public spaces can no longer be presumed to be safe, and that includes the Christmas markets that flourish across much of Europe this time of year. 

_____

Russia's ambassador to Turkey was assassinated by a lone Turkish gunman, described by officials as an off-duty police officer.

In the attack, captured in this video (warning: graphic scenes), the gunman shouted "don't forget Aleppo, don't forget Syria!"

There is no indication so far that the two countries will alter their policies towardthe war in Syria. Turkish, Russian and Iranian officials will meet in Moscow today to discuss the future of the Syrian city of Aleppo.

_____

Hours earlier, the United Nations passed a resolution to observe the evacuation of tens of thousands of civilians from Aleppo. Russia signed on only after a compromise that could allow Syrian soldiers and allied Shiite militias to block the monitors' access.

One of the civilians saved is a 7-year-old girl, above, whose Twitter posts describing life during wartime have drawn comparisons to Anne Frank.

_____

The International Monetary Fund endorsed its leader, Christine Lagarde, despite her conviction in a French court on criminal charges of negligence related to the misuse of public funds. The judge declined to impose a fine or jail time.

Republicans in Texas applauded as their electors pushed Donald J. Trump past the 270-vote threshold in the Electoral College, affirming him as the 45th president of the United States.

A concerted effort by opponents, including protests in many state capitols, failed to persuade electors to revolt against Mr. Trump.

Back Story

When is a phone call like a diplomatic life preserver?

When you're Taiwan, and on the other end of the line is President-elect Donald J. Trump, the first American leader to speak to you in 37 years. Above, President Tsai Ing-wen during the call.

"When you are small and fighting a much bigger adversary," a diplomat from Taiwan told The Times a few months ago, "you had better use your wisdom."

China considers the island, ruled by Nationalists who fled the Communist takeover in 1949, a renegade province. So when the U.S. and China opened diplomatic relations in 1979, the U.S. had to cut off Taiwan.

As China's might and markets grew, most other countries followed suit.

Taiwan is now recognized by just 22 countries, mostly tiny island nations in the Caribbean and Pacific swayed by Taipei's aid packages. Two years ago, a former Guatemalan leader admitted to accepting bribes from Taiwan for diplomatic recognition.

Taiwan's last ally in Europe is the Vatican. But there, too, officials have made overtures toward China, which has a vigorous, if partly underground, Catholic community.

For now, Nicaragua remains with Taiwan. But it may be swayed: A mainland Chinese company has pledged to create a transoceanic shipping route through it, to rival the Panama Canal.
