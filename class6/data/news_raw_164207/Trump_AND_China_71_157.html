'TARIFF'-IC DEBT We'd best tread lightly with China Dear John The Answer Man


BYLINE: JOHN CRUDELE


SECTION: All Editions; Pg. 53


LENGTH: 603 words


Dear John: I just read your response to a letter about your recent column on Trump, China and tariffs.

I agree with you, except for your take on the situation that exists between ourselves and China - "You'd be out on the street pretty quickly," you said.

Not so, sir, we've got them right where we want them financially.  

All we have to do to ruin them is renege on our debt, which quite possibly we might do anyway. So they'd better not anger us. T.D.

Dear T.D.: Seriously, that's your interpretation of how the world of finance works?

I compared the US' situation with China to that of a tenant/landlord. We owe China lots of money, and as such we've reduced the amount of flexibility we have in demanding things from that country.

You suggest that we just renege on our obligations to China.

What happens even before that decision - when such a move is just rumored - is that the US will suddenly have to pay considerable more to borrow money from everyone. And - this is no small matter - we'll have to find investors willing to take China's place at the table.

The value of US bonds will collapse - as rates rise because of the new risk in owning them - and pension funds, municipalities, the Social Security "Trust Funds" and everyone else holding American bonds will get clobbered.

I believe you might want to rethink your position on this.

There's an old saying that goes something like this: "If I owe you $100, I'm in trouble. If I owe you a million dollars, you are in trouble."

That's the situation here. China owes us plenty - and we are in trouble.

Dear John: Wouldn't the simplest way for the Labor Department to determine how many jobs were created or lost month to month be by using Social Security FICA payroll deductions paid to the system, since everyone is required to make those payments, especially employers? C.F.

Dear C.F.: You would think that would work, wouldn't you?

But then every bureaucrat who works on the Labor survey and everyone who works at the Census Bureau going house to house would be out of a job. And it would probably be considered a sin for the government to actually save money on these surveys.

CAN'T BE SCHOOLED ON SUMMER JOBS

Dear John: I attended Hudson Valley Community College and Brockport, a SUNY college, between 1966 and 1969. I can't remember how much I paid, but I earned all the money I needed for tuition, books, room and meals by working summer jobs.

That is, mid-June to mid-August.

I will bet my income against your income that a kid today can't do that. B.R.

Dear B.R.: I'm not going to take your bet - not only because I can't afford to put my salary on the line but because I agree with you.

Even if college expenses were only $10,000 a year - which they aren't - how could any kid make that kind of money (legally) in today's economy?

College costs, helped by easy-to-get student loans, have far outpaced other expenses over the years. Eventually college costs will need to come into line with reality, or a lot of schools will be going out of business and a lot of kids will be denied a college education.

And - this is very important - many kids and their families will make a very reasonable decision that jobs in the trades (plumbers, electricians, carpenters, etc.) aren't so bad after all.

I know that a lot of my salary these days is going to people who can fix things around my house that I can't. A lot of days, I wish I had entered one of those professions.

Look, I graduated from college in 1973 with very little debt, thanks to generous family members - and working summer jobs.
